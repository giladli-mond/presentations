CHAPTER 1 — THE BIG PICTURE

Three core goals, one industry

📣

Brand Awareness

Tell people you exist. Get your name in front of as many relevant people as possible. Success = impressions, reach, recall.

Top of funnel
🎯

Performance / Conversion

Get people to do something: click, sign up, buy. Every pound spent is measured against a specific outcome. Success = CPA, ROAS.

Mid / bottom of funnel
🔁

Retention / Re-engagement

Bring existing customers back. Upsell, reactivate dormant users, reduce churn. Success = repeat purchase, LTV uplift.

Loyalty / bottom of funnel

Most companies run all three simultaneously. The split depends on their growth stage: early-stage companies often over-index on performance; mature brands invest more in brand.

These three goals map roughly to the marketing funnel: Awareness (top), Consideration/Conversion (middle), Retention (bottom). Different ad types, formats, and platforms serve each goal differently.

This is called Real-Time Bidding (RTB) or Programmatic Advertising. It replaced the old model where humans negotiated ad buys directly.

The second-price auction is a Vickrey auction model — it encourages honest bidding because you only pay just above the second-highest bid, not your maximum. Header bidding later allowed publishers to run multiple auctions simultaneously to maximise yield.

CHAPTER 4 — PLATFORM DEEP DIVE

Google Ads: the intent engine

  • Search Ads — appear in Google search results when someone types a query. Charged per click (CPC). Highest purchase intent of any channel.
  • Display Ads (GDN) — banners on 2M+ websites across the Google Display Network. Great for reach and retargeting.
  • YouTube Ads — skippable/non-skippable video. Second largest search engine. Strong for awareness + consideration.
  • Shopping Ads — product images with prices in search results. Essential for e-commerce.
  • Performance Max (PMax) — AI-driven campaign type that runs across all Google channels simultaneously. Growing fast.

Google's revenue split by ad type (approx.)

Google Search is unique: users initiate. The ad answers an existing question. This is why search has the highest conversion rates of any channel.

Google's Quality Score algorithm determines ad placement. It's not just who bids highest — ad relevance, expected click-through rate, and landing page experience all factor in. A highly relevant ad can beat a higher bidder.

CHAPTER 4 — PLATFORM DEEP DIVE

Meta Ads: the targeting powerhouse

  • Core Audiences — target by demographics, location, interests, and behaviours. Built from Facebook profile data and activity.
  • Custom Audiences — upload your customer list. Meta matches to Facebook users. Also: retarget website visitors (Pixel) or app users.
  • Lookalike Audiences — Meta finds users who resemble your best customers. Often the highest-performing prospecting tool.
  • Advantage+ Audiences — Meta's AI selects the best audience automatically. Increasingly defaulted to after iOS 14 signal loss.
  • Formats — Feed, Stories, Reels, Carousel, Collection, Messenger. Creative quality matters enormously here.

Apple's iOS 14.5 (2021) destroyed Meta's tracking accuracy. Attributable conversions dropped ~30-40%. Meta's AI has been recovering, but measurement is still imperfect.

The Pixel is a JavaScript snippet you put on your website. It fires events (PageView, AddToCart, Purchase) back to Meta. This is how Meta knows who visited your site and can retarget them. Server-side Conversions API is the modern alternative that bypasses browser-level blocking.

CHAPTER 7 — METRICS & MEASUREMENT

The numbers that actually matter

CPM — Cost Per Mille
Cost to show your ad 1,000 times. Used for awareness campaigns. Typical range: $1-$50+ depending on audience quality.
CPM = (Total Spend / Impressions) × 1,000
CPC — Cost Per Click
How much you pay each time someone clicks your ad. Google Search CPCs average $1-$5 (varies wildly by industry).
CPC = Total Spend / Clicks
CTR — Click-Through Rate
What % of people who saw the ad clicked it. Search CTR averages ~3-5%. Display is typically <0.5%.
CTR = Clicks / Impressions × 100
CPA — Cost Per Acquisition
How much you spend to get one conversion (purchase, signup, lead). The primary performance metric for direct-response campaigns.
CPA = Total Spend / Conversions
ROAS — Return on Ad Spend
Revenue generated for every £1 spent on ads. A ROAS of 4x = £4 back for every £1 spent. Target varies by margin.
ROAS = Revenue / Ad Spend
LTV : CAC Ratio
Lifetime value of a customer vs. cost to acquire them. Healthy SaaS target: 3:1 or higher. Tells you if growth is sustainable.
Ratio = Customer LTV / CAC
VCR — Video Completion Rate
% of viewers who watched the full video ad. Good benchmark: 70%+ for skippable YouTube ads.
VCR = Completed Views / Total Views × 100
Frequency & Reach
Reach = unique people exposed to your ad. Frequency = average times each person saw it. Sweet spot: 3-7 exposures before fatigue sets in.
Reach × Frequency = Total Impressions
ROAS is a common but imperfect metric — it ignores margin. £4 ROAS on a 10% margin product is bad; the same ROAS on an 80% margin SaaS product is great. Always put metrics in the context of unit economics.

Most SMBs skip awareness and jump straight to performance. This works short-term but limits ceiling growth — people can't click an ad for a brand they've never heard of.

Full-funnel advertising is the gold standard but requires significant budget. Most brands start at the bottom (performance) and gradually invest up the funnel as they scale. Attribution across the funnel is notoriously hard — each stage influences the one below it.

CHAPTER 9 — TARGETING

How ads find the right person

Demographic

Age, gender, location, language, household income. The basics — available on almost every platform.

Interest / Behavioural

People who've shown interest in a topic (Meta, Google). Based on what they've liked, searched, or watched.

Intent (In-Market)

People actively researching or comparing products like yours. Google's strongest signal — search queries.

Retargeting

People who've already visited your site, used your app, or watched your content. Warm audience = higher conversion.

Lookalike / Similar

AI finds new users who resemble your best customers. Feed it your top 1,000 customers; it finds more like them.

Contextual

Place ads alongside relevant content, not based on user data. Growing again as cookies die. No privacy concerns.

First-party data is now the most valuable targeting asset. Email lists, CRM data, purchase history — these are signals no platform can take away from you.

The targeting hierarchy: intent signals are highest quality (they're in-market right now), then behavioural/interest (likely interested), then demographic (broad but scalable), then contextual (no user signal at all but privacy-safe). The ideal strategy layers multiple signals.

CHAPTER 10 — WHAT'S CHANGING

The privacy revolution is reshaping the industry

  • Third-party cookies dying — Chrome is deprecating them (delayed but coming). These cookies powered cross-site retargeting and attribution for 25 years.
  • Apple's ATT (App Tracking Transparency) — iOS 14.5 required opt-in for tracking. ~75% of users opt out. Destroyed app-level attribution accuracy.
  • GDPR / CCPA regulations — EU and US privacy laws restrict data collection and require consent. Heavy fines for violations.
  • AI compensating — Platforms are using AI to model conversions they can no longer measure directly. Meta's Advantage+, Google's Smart Bidding, PMax all lean on this.
  • First-party data as moat — Brands with strong email lists, CRMs, and logged-in users will win. Everyone else will pay more for less signal.

Data clean rooms (Google PAIR, Meta CAPI) let brands match first-party data to platform data without sharing raw records.

The cookieless future is creating a massive consolidation toward walled gardens (Google, Meta, Amazon, TikTok) because they have their own logged-in audience data. The open web is most affected. Contextual advertising is experiencing a renaissance as a result.

CHAPTER 11 — ADS IN A CAMPAIGNS CONTEXT

How email marketing intersects with ads

  • Lead gen ads — paid ads drive traffic to landing pages where people give their email in exchange for something (discount, guide, trial). Email list = product of ad spend.
  • Email retargeting via ads — upload your email list to Meta/Google. Run ads to subscribers who haven't opened emails. Multi-channel reinforcement.
  • CRM-synced audiences — connect your CRM to ad platforms. Automatically exclude churned customers, target trial users with upgrade ads, suppress paid plans from acquisition campaigns.
  • Post-send retargeting — send an email, then show ads to everyone who opened but didn't click. Layers channels for higher conversion.
  • Measurement challenge — if a customer gets an email AND sees a Facebook ad AND clicks a Google ad, who gets credit? Multi-touch attribution is still unsolved.
This is where email marketing and paid advertising overlap directly. Platforms like monday campaigns, Klaviyo, or Braze increasingly offer direct integrations with Meta and Google for CRM-synced audiences. The trend is toward unified customer engagement, not siloed channels.

CHAPTER 14 — THE HARD PART

Attribution: who gets the credit?

A customer sees a YouTube ad on Monday. Googles the brand on Wednesday. Gets an email Thursday. Clicks a retargeting Facebook ad on Friday. Buys on Saturday.

Which channel "caused" the purchase?

LAST-CLICK
Facebook gets 100%
The old default. Misleading — ignores everything that built intent.
DATA-DRIVEN
AI distributes credit
Google's model (GA4 default). Better, but black-box and Google-biased.
BEST PRACTICE
Incrementality testing
Run holdout groups: turn off a channel for a segment and measure the delta. True causal measurement.
Attribution is the central unsolved problem in digital marketing. Different attribution models lead to very different budget allocation decisions. Last-click systematically under-values upper-funnel spend. Incrementality testing (holdout experiments) is the gold standard but requires scale.
