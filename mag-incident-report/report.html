Section 1

Executive Summary

On Friday 13 March 2026, a cascading failure in the campaigns email pipeline sent approximately 1.6 million duplicate emails across 35 customer accounts. The most severely impacted customer, MAG (Motor Auction Group), had a campaign intended for 4,290 contacts result in over 1.3 million emails. Some contacts received the same email thousands of times, with one case reaching 24,000 duplicates.

The technical incident, while serious, was identified and resolved within hours by the engineering team. The larger failure was in the customer experience that followed:

  • MAG received incorrect information from support agents, including being told that monday.com could not stop the emails
  • It took over 13 hours for the technical support team to acknowledge the issue was a bug
  • The customer opened two separate support tickets over 3 days because responses were generic and unhelpful
  • The tech support team did not contact the product team at any point during the crisis. The product team only became aware through its own internal escalation on Friday evening
  • MAG's account was unassigned with no AM or CSM. The previous AM was providing bridge support voluntarily
  • This follows the AHA Advising churn ($59,900 ARR lost, February 2026), where the same support gaps were present
  • The product team had already flagged the lack of communication with the tech support team in the last week of February 2026, and raised the account coverage gap with the CSM team on 4 March. No action was taken on either front
  • Had tech support highlighted this case to the product team on Friday, one of the engineers would have dealt with it personally, and the customer impact could have been significantly smaller

The customer's business has suffered significant, potentially irreversible damage: 4,000+ subscribers lost, sending domains blocked by major providers, staff taking sick leave due to abuse, and emergency board meetings convened. Legal counsel has been consulted. The customer is considering switching providers and has engaged their own legal team.

Section 3

Commercial Impact & Customer Experience

Section summary: MAG is an Enterprise customer (~$16.6K ARR) that added campaigns just 6 days before the incident, was forecast to upgrade, and had no assigned AM or CSM. During a crisis that caused severe business damage, the customer received incorrect guidance from support, waited 3+ days for a meaningful response, and had to find senior monday.com staff through LinkedIn. Throughout the incident, the tech support team did not contact the product team — the product team only became aware through its own internal escalation on Friday evening. Had support flagged this to the product team on Friday, an engineer would have dealt with it personally, and the impact could have been significantly smaller. This mirrors the AHA Advising case from one month earlier ($59.9K ARR churned), where the same support gaps were present. The product team had flagged the lack of communication with the tech support team in the last week of February, and raised the account coverage gap with the CSM team on 4 March. No action was taken on either front before the MAG incident occurred.

3.5 Legal Consultation — 18 March 2026

Legal counsel (Aaron) was consulted on 18 March. Key guidance:

  • Focus should be on relationship repair, not compensation framing. Incentives (AI credits, renewal discount) can be offered as a token of good faith, not as compensation for the bug

Section 4

Technical Incident & Product Response

Section summary: A bug deployed on Friday morning caused duplicate email sending across the campaigns pipeline. The issue escalated from 2x to 300x duplicates as the system degraded. On-call engineers were alerted within minutes, declared an incident, and deployed an initial fix within 30 minutes. However, the system remained degraded, and it took 2.5 hours from incident declaration to fully disabling email sending. During this window, MAG and other customers sent campaigns. Six fixes were deployed by 10:41 PM, and sending was restored. Additional safeguards were deployed the following week. The engineering response was fast. The critical gap was that the tech support team did not contact the product team throughout the incident. The product team became aware only through its own internal escalation at 9:35 PM — over 7 hours after the customer's first contact. Had this been flagged earlier, an engineer would have handled it directly.

  • Impact window: 12:52 PM to 3:59 PM (3 hours 7 minutes)

4.3 Fixes Deployed

Immediate (Friday evening)

  • Idempotency guards to prevent duplicate processing
  • Timeout adjustments to prevent cascading retries
  • Load shedding to manage pipeline pressure

Follow-up (18 March)

  • Additional safeguards to prevent duplicate email sending

Planned

  • Throughput-based alerting to catch anomalies faster
  • Rate limiting across the pipeline
  • Better logging for workflow endpoints

4.4 Key Technical Gap

The system was known to be degraded from 12:52 PM, and the incident was declared at 1:25 PM. Yet email sending was not disabled until 3:59 PM, a gap of 2 hours and 34 minutes. During this window, MAG (1:43 PM) and other customers sent campaigns into a broken pipeline with no warning.

There is currently no mechanism to:

  • Warn customers that the system is degraded
  • Prevent or block campaign sending during a declared incident
  • Alert the support team that an active incident is in progress so they can inform customers

5.3 Systemic Gaps Requiring Executive Action

  1. Escalation path from support to product teams: No mechanism exists for support to escalate critical product incidents to product teams in real time. In both the MAG and AHA cases, the tech support team did not contact the product team. The product team had explicitly flagged this communication gap in the last week of February. Had support contacted the product team on Friday afternoon, an engineer would have handled the MAG case directly, and the customer impact could have been significantly smaller. This is the single most important gap
  2. Severity-1 SLA: No defined SLA for critical customer incidents. MAG waited 3+ days for a meaningful response. The support team lacked both the tooling and the authority to treat this as an emergency
  3. Emergency tooling for support: Support agents could not stop active email sending and gave incorrect guidance because they lacked both the tools and the product knowledge. Agents need the ability to escalate directly to engineering for product-critical incidents
  4. Incident safeguards: No mechanism to warn customers or block campaign sending during a declared incident. The 2.5-hour gap between incident declaration and disabling sending allowed MAG and others to send campaigns into a broken pipeline
  5. Account coverage: Enterprise accounts with no AM or CSM create invisible risk. MAG was unassigned. The product team flagged the communication gap with tech support in the last week of February, and met with the CSM team on 4 March to flag the account coverage gap and request action items. No structural changes were made on either front in the 9 days before the MAG incident
